3.       Difference Between Native and Non-Native Bees

                         

             There are more than 20,000 known wild bee species and an estimated 30,000 honey bee species around the world, though most of the available data seems to be from North America and Europe it is not for a lack of trying. (S.G. Potts, 2016) (Council, 2007) There are bees that are social, like bumble bees, that live together and share in the baby-rearing and provisioning of foods, and there are others that are solitary, which live and raise their brood alone. (Moisset, 2011) Even though there are many ways to segment and categorize bees, for the sake of this whitepaper, we will view bees as either native or non-native bees.

               

            3.1      Non-Native Bees

              Western honey bees (Apis mellifera) are native to Africa, Europe, and Western Asia and are usually referred to as non-native bees in other locations around the world. Nearly 17,000 species of honey bees have been formally described, and as many as 30,000 are estimated worldwide. (Council, 2007) The Apis mellifera, is often cited as the honey bee even though there are at least eight other honey bee species, which all produce honey. For example, there is the giant honey bee (Apis dorsata), the dwarf honey bee (Apis florea), and the Bornean honey bee (Apis koschevnikovi) only exist as wild populations. However, because the eight other honey bees live in mild tropical climates, they have no need to store large amounts of nectar, their main energy source, because they can find enough flowers to meet their energy needs throughout the year. Whereas the Apis mellifera has evolved over regions with limited flower production, or periods of it (winter, rainy days, etc), that lead to storing large amounts of nectar that dehydrate over time, turning into honey, which is then a source of energy for the bees year-round. (Requier, 2019)

              A hive is the bees home and a colony is a queen and all of her worker bees located in one hive. And since honey bees are one of only a few species that live in a hive, this allows for them to be moved around from location to location efficiently. Production of honey, the large number of bees in one hive, and excellent crop pollination partly explain why honey bees have travelled all over the world and are considered non-native bees in some areas. And it is estimated that there are 81 million bee hives around the world producing 1.6 million tons of honey annually. (S.G. Potts, 2016)      

              The honey bee is a social organism that lives in hives which can contain in upwards of 50,000 bees that are primarily worker bees (females) further subdivided by castes, but all under the direction of a single queen (Agency, 2012). The individual bee cannot function independent of the hive for prolonged periods; therefore, the functional unit of the honey bee is the colony itself. (Agency, 2012)  Given that the hive is composed of thousands of bees, it has been likened to a superorganism. (Agency, 2012)

              Interestingly, there were no honey bees in America before European settlers brought hives from Europe. (Moisset, 2011)  These resourceful animals promptly managed to escape from domestication and as they had done for millennia in Europe and Asia, honey bees formed swarms and set up nests in hollow trees and other areas. (Moisset, 2011)  The honey bee, remarkable as it is, does not know how to pollinate tomato or eggplant flowers and it does very poorly compared to native bees when pollinating many native plants, such as pumpkins, cherries, blueberries, and cranberries. (Moisset, 2011)

 

              3.2      Native Bees

 

               Native bees come in a wide variety of shapes, sizes, and colors and are as varied in their life styles as well. From the seasons they prefer, the flowers they visit, and the nests they build, there are so many descriptions that it would exceed what is required here. In the United States, native bees provide pollination to roughly 80% of flowering plants and aside from cuckoo bees, all bees build nests.  (Moisset, 2011)

              Solitary is a term that is used when referencing native bees at times, and it turns out that of the known bee species, roughly 98% are solitary bees where they do not live in large groups, with a few examples being the mason bee, plasterer bee, blue orchard bee, squash bee, teddy bear bee, and the carpenter bee. (Lab, 2021) Approximately 70% of female solitary bees prepare their own nests in the ground, while the remaining nests are usually prepared in cracks in wood. (Lab, 2021)  And in either location, the females prepare her brood cells, gathers nectar and pollen into each cell, and then lays her eggs in those cells and provides no further help to the next generation after they are born. (Lab, 2021)

             

 

4.       Status of Bee Pollinators

          4.1      Data

              Even though there is a consensus to the lack of global bee data available, there are several databases that attempt to be a resource. For example, the Bee Database Project at the American Museum of Natural History and the World Bee Checklist by the Integrated Taxonomic Information System provide valuable information on bees around the world that is beneficial on many levels. Though comprehensively, there is still a lack of data, and consequently, knowledge of pollinator diversity still remains poor. To date, the primary cause of this is insufficient reliable occurrence data and the corresponding analytical and taxonomic expertise required to identify the bee species. For example, there are millions of museum specimens that await identification, or even formal description, and remain inaccessible to researchers, which could be of valuable information upon inspection. (Orr, et al., 2021)

              Because there is a lack of global data available to utilize, it will be part of our mission to assist in filling in those knowledge gaps. For example, public records (Table 2) illustrate that the United States has over 60% of the sampling points with the state of California having more than double the number of records of any country (except Sweden). (Orr, et al., 2021) The best sampled countries in Africa, Asia, and South America only represent less than 4% of all data points in public data. (Orr, et al., 2021) Only 12% of countries have over 95% of their land area sampled, while contrastingly, almost 15% of countries have under 5% of their land area sampled, and 55% have under 25% of their land area sampled. (Orr, et al., 2021) As it turns out, this equates to 90% of North Asia, 86% of South Asia, 82% of the Middle East, and 79% of Africa and Eurasia are completely unsampled. (Orr, et al., 2021) Globally, this means that some of the most species-rich countries have the least available data. For example, China and Mexico are two of the most species-rich countries, 6th and 2nd respectively, though only have 7% and 3% of the total amount of data point samples. (Orr, et al., 2021)

              Regarding data, the above paragraphs highlight some of the areas that BeeKeeper will attempt to be useful and meaningful. In order for anyone to understand the problem, we have to learn the data, though by learning of the lack of data above, this partly guides our mission towards assisting in the collection of data globally. More of this will be mentioned further along in the whitepaper, though our goal is to assist in having a globally comprehensive database of the important aspects of all bee species in each region of the world.

              4.2      Pollination

            Some bees are generalists and use pollen from a wide variety of flowering plants, though there are others that depend on specific plants for their pollen, and nutrition needs.

Over two-thirds of the earths food is available because of the pollination of plants, flowers, and vegetables by bees. (Claire Kremen, 2002) Pollination is the process of pollen being transferred from the anthers of male plants to the stigmata of female plants so that seeds can be produced from the newly fertilized female plant. (Sukumaran, Khanduri, & Sharma, 2020) This simple action is a significant role in agriculture and is a cornerstone of crop production even though its taking place on accident. Honey bees rely dominantly on nectar and pollen for food, and a little on oils and honeydews, and it turns out that the dehydration of nectar is what creates honey. (Requier, 2019)

              The majority of solitary bees are polylectic (i.e., collect pollen from numerous plant species), while a smaller number are oligolectic (use a narrow range of plants) and very few are monolithic (use pollen from only a single plant species). (Mark A Hall, 2020) Although native bee species are often more efficient pollinators than are honey bees on a flower-by-flower basis, honey bees are, for many reasons, the pollinator of choice for most North American crops. (Council, 2007) Honey bee colonies are able to muster large numbers of pollinators when they are needed for late winter and early spring blooms, as well as throughout the rest of the growing season. And generally speaking, honey bee can pollinate many agricultural crops, including almond and blueberry and especially because it forages over long distances (up to 14 km or 8.6 miles from its nest). In addition, honey bees exhibit sophisticated communication, which increases foraging efficiency, and floral constancy; individuals repeatedly visit a single plant species during each foraging trip and can recruit nestmates to flowers of that species. (Council, 2007)

             

              4.3      Geography

            The concentration of bees around the world is diverse and so is the vegetation. Pollination of crops is seasonal at times and while bees live according to their own schedule this creates gaps in production for some of our crops. Even though there has been research surrounding the challenges of bees, not all plants like all bees and the same goes the other way around, not all bees like every one of the plants they visit, and the more we know about this, the better. When we fail there are opportunities to learn, for example when eusocial yellowjacket wasps were introduced to the Hawaiian Islands, they had a negative impact on vegetation. (Keng-Lou James Hung, 2019) We seem to understand how pollination works though at times we lack the understanding of how our pollinators interact with our own environments.

              In 2002, a survey of feral honey bee colonies in Arnot Forest, New York was conducted and found that the number of honey bee colonies was about the same in 1978 as it was in 2002. However, another study was conducted on the spread of varroa mites within Californias population of feral honey bees and in 1990, a sample of bees from 208 colonies located in feral hives revealed no varroa mites, though in 1993, a survey of 124 of the same feral hives revealed 75 percent of these colonies no longer existed, and all surviving colonies were infested with varroa. The proximity of these feral colonies relative to commercial apiaries suggest that the mites moved from commercial to feral colonies. (Council, 2007) This is an example of the possible changes between bee populations and how they can be so drastic.

              Europe hosts about 10% of worldwide bee diversity although the continent only represents 7% of global terrestrial habitats and the most prominent and diverse family of bees is the Apidae (561 species), which includes the Honey bee and the bumblebees. (Nieto, 2014) The difference in geography highlights the importance of more research, for example, in Belgium, several native bees are under threat or even disappeared locally, but their populations has remained constant in other areas of Europe. (Tanda, 2021)

            4.4      Economically

            More than $500 billion worth of global food production was directly contributed by pollinators while the volume of agricultural production has increased by 300% in the last 50 years. (Nations, 2016) Bee pollination is responsible for roughly 9.5% of the total economic value of agriculture production used directly for food in the world. (Klein, et al., 2006) In the USA alone, pollination results in $16 billion annually. (Khalifa, et al., 2021) In countries that grow cash crops such as coffee, cocoa, almonds, and soybeans, they have a much greater reliance on bee pollination in agriculture at a large scale comparatively. (Khalifa, et al., 2021) Financially, in the United States, honey bees are responsible for the pollination of up to $12 billion in food. (Khalifa, et al., 2021)

              On average, pollinator-dependent crops have higher prices than non-pollinator-dependent crops and the distribution of these monetary benefits is not uniform, with the greatest additional production occurring in parts of Eastern Asia, the Middle East, Mediterranean Europe, and North America. The additional monetary output linked to pollination services accounts for 5% - 15% of total crop output in different United Nations regions, with the greatest contributions in the Middle East, South Asia, and East Asia. In the absence of animal pollination, changes in global crop supplies could increase prices to consumers and reduce profits to producers, resulting in a potential annual net loss of economic welfare of $160 billion-$191 billion globally to crop consumers and producers and a further $207 billion-$497 billion to producers and consumers in other, non-crop markets (e.g., non-crop agriculture, forestry and food processing)

              A significant source of the economic importance of honey bees is that apiculturethe management of honey beesis a highly developed discipline that has made bees and beekeeping equipment widely available. For example, Latin America produces 28% of the global honey supply with 7.7 million managed honey bee colonies while Argentina leads the global list of countries in terms of the honey market, with more than $200,000 dollars intake related to exports of honey in 2013 (subtracting the imports) and the western honey bee is not-native to Latin America. (Requier, 2019)

 

5        Problem Analysis        

 

              Honey bees have been used to provide pollination services for crops in bloom in extensive areas. And since honey bees can be concentrated in very high densities, they can be transported by truck to any location at any time crops are in bloom. And because honey bees can be cared for and maintained by humans, they are buffered to some extent from declines in environmental quality, though on the other and, because of the need to manage honey bees, there has been more research conducted on honey bees. (Council, 2007)

              There are concerns with certain bee species going extinct, and currently, roughly 16.5% of vertebrate pollinators are threatened with extinction globally. (Nations, 2016) A declining pollinator species can hasten its own demise, because a decline in numbers is frequently accompanied by a decline in genetic variability, via genetic drift, which increases the likelihood that populations and species will become extinct. (Tepedino, 2001)

              There are several factors effecting the decline of bees and they vary from arthropod pests, disease, poor nutrition, decreases in the diversity of food resources, loss of habitat, lack of genetic diversity, and pesticides. (Committee, 2013) Because of the varied challenges that bees face, it is important for us to understand each of those challenges at depth so that we can act accordingly and attempt to increase bee populations around the world through specific actions based on the current bee challenges. Because there is no one specific cause or combination of factors that are affecting the bees and causing their decline, the approach to the solutions should be as broad and varied as the potential causations.

             

              5.1      Parasites/Viruses/Bacterial Diseases

            Non-Native Bees

In the United States, data from the Department of Agricultures National Agricultural Statistics Service (NASS) reveal declines in the number of honey bee colonies producing honey during 19471972 and 19891996 (USDA-NASS, 1995, 1999, 2004a, 2005, 2006a). Overall, the number of managed colonies dropped from 5.9 million in 1947 to 2.6 million in 19962004 and that number fell again in 2005 to 2.4 million. The decline from 1985 to 1996 is likely linked to the occurrence of the tracheal mite, Acarapis woodi (first detected in 1984) and to the Varroa destructor mite (first detected in 1987). (Council, 2007) The varroa mite has been studied over the course of the last fifty years, but the conclusion that the varroa mite feeds exclusively on honey bees has not been established until recently. (Samuel D. Ramsey, 2019)

Separately, though overlapping, in the United States during 2006-2009, one-third of all honey bees were lost and without a significant idea of what caused it, which became known as Colony Collapse Disorder. (Mullin CA, 2010) According to the Environmental Protection Agency, only 50% of the colonies lost during the winter of 2006-2007 had symptoms that were identifiable to their loss, like the varroa mite, meaning that other half of the colonies lost exhibited no known causes for their loss. (Agency, epa.gov, 2021) There have many theories about Colony Collapse Disorder, and even though there are no answers as of now, this has increased research in other areas of concern for bees, like the newly discovered Israeli Acute Paralysis virus and the gut parasite Nosema. (Agency, epa.gov, 2021)

The American foulbrood (AFB) is a serious infectious disease of honey bees caused by the spore-forming bacteria Paenibacillus larvae and found worldwide.  (Council, 2007)  AFB is one of the most widespread diseases affecting honey bee brood and is the most destructive.  (Council, 2007) The disease does not pose any health risks to people, but it wreaks havoc among bees and severe outbreaks can weaken or kill entire colonies. (Council, 2007)

This is not meant to be exhaustive or conclusive regarding the parasites, viruses, and bacteria affecting honey bees, though more will be added as information becomes available.

                            Native Bees

Among the thousands of species of non-native bees, bumble bees seem to be amongst the highest in regard to the amount of research conducted on a non-native bee species. A major cause of decline in native bumble bees appears to be recently introduced non-native protozoan parasites, including Nosema bombi and Crithidia bombi, probably from commercial bumble bees imported from Europe for greenhouse pollination. The bees frequently harbor pathogens and their escape from greenhouses can lead to infections in native species. Disease, notably chalkbrood (caused by the fungal pathogen Ascosphaera aggregata), also has harmed populations of Megachile rotundata, the alfalfa leafcutter bee, in the United States. (Council, 2007)

In New Brunswick, Canada the blueberry crop was nearly wiped out even though the plants appeared healthy. Unfortunately, it was determined that the program to control the spruce worm in nearby forests had almost eliminated the native bees when this was brought to light with blueberry farmers succeeding in the Supreme Court of Canada. Ultimately, the Canadian Government placed restrictions on the use of pesticides, but after several years the local populations of native bees were restored and the blueberry crops were once again plentiful. (Moisset, 2011)

This is not an exhaustive list, though as more information becomes available, it will be added here to bring attention to the parasites/viruses/bacterial diseases affecting native bees around the world.

Ecology

Ecologically, bees can be separated into two groups based on the relative length of their tongues, called proboscides, which are used to gather nectar in flowers. (Moisset, 2011) At first glance this may seem to be irrelevant or unnecessary data, though it is important in partly understanding the relationships between bees and the flowers or crops they pollinate. However, they are difficult to measure so it is rarely included in the ecological analysis of bees. (Daniel P. Cariveau, 2016) We dont have all the necessary data to categorize bees ecologically according to their proboscides, though, this aspect of bees is worth mentioning here as it illustrates their importance.

The ecological impact to bees could be the most important aspect affecting their decline, Pesticides, insecticides, herbicides, and fungicides have both a direct and indirect negative effect on pollinators, though it is essential to search for positive affects as well. Since honey bees are responsible for a significant portion of total crop pollination, combined with the Colony Collapse Disorder, there has been an increased level of research done on honey bees, and rightfully so, though more research is needed on native bees. (Maini, 2010)

Landscapes all around the world are changed for many reasons, from Mountain-Top-Removal in search of coal to dredging rivers for larger port capacities. Natural wetlands have been manipulated for mosquito control, and these activities can affect pollinator populations because wetlands can provide important bee forage. The most widespread current manipulation programs of natural wetlands are probably salt marsh water management techniques that are designed to control salt marsh mosquitoes by minimizing water retention in puddles in the high marsh and allowing fish access to ponds and puddles to control larvae. (Howard S. Ginsberg, 2017) To our knowledge, there have been no studies that specifically quantified the effects of open marsh water management practices on the availability of floral resources for pollinators. (Howard S. Ginsberg, 2017) Planting of ornamentals, while beneficial to some pollinators, can sometimes serve as mosquito larval habitat. For example, Ae. aegypti readily breeds in artificial containers of water. (Howard S. Ginsberg, 2017)

Habitat manipulation for tick control includes clearing of leaf litter (Schulze et al. 1995), removing lower vegetation and ground cover (Milne 1948, Hubalek et al. 2006), opening the tree canopy (Mount 1981), and controlled burning (Wilson 1986, Stafford et al. 1998). These practices would make conditions less favorable for some plant species, but more favorable for others, so the effects on pollinator resources would depend on local conditions and the specific manipulations applied. Similarly, because of the diversity of nesting habitat preferences by different bee species, these practices would vary in their effects on quality of bee nesting habitat.

In some instances, pesticides are applied to crops when the vegetation itself is blooming, and by default, increases the exposure that bees have to the pesticides. (Heller, 2020) As Ginsberg  2017 states below:

The risks to pollinators presented by pesticides used for disease vector control depend upon the toxicities, targeting of applications, and environmental fates of those pesticides. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency characterizes pesticides in terms of relative toxicity to different ecological receptors, including birds, aquatic organisms (fish and invertebrates), wild mammals, and nontarget insects. With respect to honey bees, the categories are 1) highly toxic when the acute toxicity value (LD50 from acute contact testing, OPP 850.3020) is< 2 mg/bee, 2) moderately toxic when the LD50 is between 2 and 10.99 mg/bee, and 3) practically nontoxic when the LD50 11 mg/bee. The organophosphates chlorpyrifos, malathion, and naled, and most of the pyrethroids and pyrethrins, along with imidacloprid and fipronil, are categorized as highly toxic to honey bees with direct exposure (Table 2). A categorization of practically nontoxic was assigned to the bacterial toxin from a subspecies of Bacillus thuringiensis (kurstaki). The toxicity of methoprene to honey bees was not reported by the USEPA. While the honey bee acute toxicity data for select insecticides summarized in Table 2 might be useful as a guide or rule of thumb for toxicity to a local bee community, recent research has shown that while modes of action are the same among bee species, toxicities vary greatly. For instance, Sanchez-Bayo and Goka (2014) reported that honey bees are considerably more sensitive to permethrin than bumble bees (Bombus spp.) but less sensitive to carbaryl. Biddinger et al. (2013) found honey bees to be far less sensitive to the neonicotinoid insecticide, acetamiprid, than Osmia cornifrons (Radoszkowski), but sensitivity to the neonicotinoid imidacloprid was reversed, the honey bee being far more sensitive than O. lignaria Say. Thus, while the large volume of accumulated data on honey bee toxicity to insecticides is useful, these data cannot be directly applied to the native bee community or to other pollinators in North America. Toxicities of insecticides to native bees and other wild insect pollinators are important areas for future research

(Nieto, 2014) Each bee species requires their own unique ecosystem of what it takes for them thrive and that means getting to know the bees personally. In order to provide any type of solutions for the thousands of bees around the world, we have to understand them at the level needed to affect change.

          There is a need for more research to understand the importance of bee dwelling sites and the components effecting nesting and their locations by various bee species. (Tanda, 2021)

               

          Pollination Plant Management

              The number of visits and the aggregate effects of various bee species influence not only the quantity of crops produced but also their quality. Studying plant vegetation and bee interaction is important, and deservedly so, especially after studies like one in sub-Saharan Africa, which is considered the main producer of cotton, found that bee pollination increases the cotton yield to 62% compared with an estimated 37% without bee pollination. From apples in Pakistan, strawberries in Germany, coconuts in Mexico, avocados in Central America, and to tomatoes in Brazil, we have learned that bees increase both the quality and quality after being introduced as pollinators. (Mark A Hall, 2020)

          Farming

          There are farmers all over the world who are dependent on bees to pollinate their crops and in some instances, there are farmers that do not have any local bees surrounding their crops and must bring in beekeepers in order to pollinate their crops. For example, there are almond farmers in the United States paying up to $300 per acre for beekeepers to come pollinate their crops every FebruaryWhether its from a lack of resources, education, or time, bringing in beekeepers cost farmers, and subsequently consumers, roughly $5 - $14 billion

 USDA cost-sharing, land retirement, and production stewardship programs should be available to producers of all commoditiesfruits, nuts, and vegetablesthat depend on pollinators.  The NRCS should target new hiring of personnel whose expertise is in biological sciences, especially ecology and natural-area management.

Based on a study of the figures published by the National Agricultural Statistics Service of the USDA, interviews with beekeepers, extensionists and researchers, we estimate that there were 2,500,000 colonies rented for pollination purposes in 1998. This is up from 2,035,000 in 1989

          Nutrition

          Changes in floral composition shape nutrient availability in inhabited areas, and bee larvae need food rich in body-building nutrients to develop into adults. Adult food, mainly composed of energy-rich nectar, differs from larval food, mainly composed of pollen, and adult bees forage on different plant species for nectar and pollen. Defining bee-friendly plants based on the quantities of food produced, and on the visitation rates of adult pollinating insects leads to the planting of bee habitats with poor-quality food for larvae, which limits their growth and development, and negatively affects the population.

          Genetics

          Extinction

          According to the Center for Biological Diversity, in the United States of the more than 1,400 different native bee species roughly 48% of the native bee populations are secure and stable, while roughly 27% is Declining and 24% is Declining and Threatened of going extinct. In Europe, as of 2014, 9% of bees are threatened with extinction, however there is insufficient data on 1,101 other bee species to make this determination. (Nieto, 2014)

              Indirect Problems

Value of Non-native Bee Pollination

              The Western honey bee, Apis mellifera, is often valued as the most important pollinator to our crops globally for a variety of reasons, though a few are because there can be between 10,000 and 30,000 bees in one colony and because they stay busy year round pollinating. There are 17,000 species of bees that have been formally described, but it is estimated that there are up to 30,000 species of honey bees worldwide (Michener, 2000) In North America honey bees are responsible for pollinating over 100 commercial crops and billions of dollars in value each year. According to the United States Department of Agriculture, there were 2.7 million honey bee colonies that produced 147 million lbs. of honey for over $299 million in 2020 alone.  

              Bees are responsible for 34% of pollination service demands in the United Kingdom. (Khalifa, et al., 2021) In Egypt, when compared to other insects, honey bees have been shown to significantly improve the seed percentage and seed yield in onions. (Ewies & EL-Sahhar, 1977)

 

Value of Native-Bee Pollination

              Approximately 239 bumble bee species are known worldwide (Williams, 1998), 49 of them in the United States

Research shows that up to 30% of all crops, like tomatoes, squash, cucumbers, peppers, cherries, and apples, are dependent on being pollinated by native bees only, meaning, that if native bees were to disappear, so would some of the food we eat. About 20%-45% of native bees are pollen specialists, meaning that they use only pollen from one species (or genus) of plants. If that plant is removed, the bee goes away and if bees are removed, the plant doesn't reproduce. Some of the native bees are specialists on the very plants that we use for food, including squashes, pumpkins, gourds, and the annual sunflower.

Indirect Benefits of Pollination

Insect-pollinated legumes have the ability to collect nitrogen from the air, store it in the roots, and ultimately leave it to enrich the soil for other plants. Without this beneficial effect, soils not fertilized by processed minerals would soon be depleted and become economically unproductive.

The animal products we consume contribute about an equal amount to our diet as plants do. (Guidry 1964) These include beef, pork, poultry, lamb, and dairy products--derived one way or another from insect-pollinated legumes such as alfalfa, clover, lespedeza, and trefoil. (Guidry 1964) More than half of the world's diet of fats and oils comes from oilseeds--coconuts, cotton, oil palm, olives, peanuts, rape, soybeans, and sunflower (Guidry 1964). Many of these plants are dependent upon or benefited by insect pollination and when these sources, the animal and plant products, are considered, it appears that perhaps one-third of our total diet is dependent, directly or indirectly, upon insect-pollinated plants. (Guidry 1964) Specifically, one example is the alfalfa seed, a bee-pollinated crop with an annual value of $109 million (direct effect), is used to produce hay for livestock forage that is valued at $4.6 billion per year (indirect effect). (Calderone, 2000)

In other ways, pollinators contribute to crops that provide biofuels, fibers, medicines, forage for livestock, candles, musical instruments, and even construction materials. (Nations, 2016) And there are even some anti-bacterial, anti-fungal, and anti-diabetic agents derived from honey, illustrating the dynamic value bees have to us all. (S.G. Potts, 2016)

Not only do they pollinate most of our flowering plants, their bodies feed other wildlife and their ground-nesting behaviors aerate and enrich soils. (Moisset, 2011)

Ecological Plan of Action

         

In the past, beekeepers could treat their bee colonies with antibiotics to control American foulbrood without a veterinarians supervision because the drugs used to be available over-the-counter. However, as of January 1, 2017, beekeepers must involve their veterinarian because the drugs are now available only by or on the order of a licensed veterinarian.

This regulatory change to require a veterinarians supervision is an important piece of FDAs overall strategy to promote the judicious use of antibiotics in food-producing animals in an effort to reduce antibiotic resistance. (Antibiotic resistance occurs after bacteria are exposed to an antibiotic and then become resistant to the drugs effects. This means that the antibiotic, and similar antibiotics, will no longer work against those bacteria.)

              In Canada, the citizens of Guelph, Ontario have created the worlds first Pollinator Park at the Eastview landfill site. In part, they were inspired by the experience with the blueberry pollination disaster, though they are turning an ordinary landfill, about 100 acres, into an oasis for bees and other pollinators with the right plantings and proper habitat for nesting. (Moisset, 2011)
